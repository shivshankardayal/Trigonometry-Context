\chapter{Summation of Trigonometric Series}

In the study of trigonometric functions, one eventually encounters objects
that are not functions in the everyday sense---they are \quotation{aggregates}
of oscillations, woven together across infinitely many frequencies.
These trigonometric series form an approximation ideal, a structured
superposition in which infinitely many tiny oscillations cooperate to
construct finite phenomena. From the hum of a vibrating string to the flicker
of light waves, such aggregates appear everywhere in mathematical physics.

Our purpose in this chapter is to explore lesser-trodden territory of
summation techniques—classical, modern, and obscure. The \quotation{esoteric
theory} here reveals the deeper unity beneath familiar formulas.

\section{The Canonical Trigonometric Series}

We begin with the classical periodic expansion:

\startformula
S(x) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \bigl( a_n \cos nx + b_n \sin nx \bigr),
\stopformula

and its complex counterpart:

\startformula
S(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{inx}.
\stopformula

The partial sums

\startformula
S_N(x) = \sum_{|n|\le N} c_n e^{inx}
\stopformula

raise the fundamental question: How should one understand the limit
$N\to\infty$? Pointwise? Uniformly? Distributionally? Or through a
summation process?

\section{Abel Summation: Dissolving Oscillation into Continuity}

One of the most elegant techniques is Abel summation:

\startformula
S_r(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n r^{|n|} e^{inx}, \qquad 0<r<1.
\stopformula

As $r\to 1^{-}$, the high frequencies return, and the original series is
recovered in a controlled manner.

This leads to the Poisson kernel:

\startformula
P_r(\theta) = \frac{1-r^2}{1 - 2r\cos\theta + r^2},
\stopformula

and the harmonic representation:

\startformula
S_r(x) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} S(t)P_r(x-t)\,dt.
\stopformula

Thus, Abel summation smothers oscillation and then allows us to recover
the boundary behavior of a harmonic function on the unit disk.

\section{Cesàro Summation and Fejér's Mystical Smoothing}

Instead of multiplying coefficients by $r^n$, Cesàro summation averages
partial sums:

\startformula
\sigma_N(x) = \frac{1}{N+1}\sum_{k=0}^{N} S_k(x).
\stopformula

Its kernel, the Fejér kernel, is

\startformula
K_N(\theta) = \frac{1}{N+1}
\left(\frac{\sin\frac{(N+1)\theta}{2}}{\sin\frac{\theta}{2}}\right)^2.
\stopformula

This kernel is nonnegative and integrates to $2\pi$, making
Cesàro summation a purified, well-behaved form of convergence—one that
eliminates oscillatory defects such as the Gibbs phenomenon.

\section{Principal Value and Divergent Sine Series}

Consider the divergent series:

\startformula
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \sin nx.
\stopformula

Although divergent in the classical sense, one may assign it a
\quotation{principal value}. Using the identity:

\startformula
\sum_{n=1}^{N} \sin nx =
\frac{\sin\frac{Nx}{2}\,\sin\frac{(N+1)x}{2}}{\sin\frac{x}{2}},
\stopformula

and taking a symmetric limit, one obtains the distributional identity:

\startformula
\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \sin nx = \frac{1}{2}\cot\frac{x}{2}.
\stopformula

Here the \quotation{sum} becomes a singular generalized function: an
analytic echo left by infinite oscillation.

\section{Ramanujan Summation and Trigonometric Quasi-Values}

Ramanujan summation assigns values to wildly divergent series. For example:

\startformula
1 - \cos x + \cos 2x - \cos 3x + \cdots
\stopformula

has the Ramanujan sum:

\startformula
\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{2}\cot\frac{x}{2}.
\stopformula

The method consists of subtracting the integral trend from finite sums,
leaving a subtle residue of oscillation. Such quasi-values transcend
ordinary convergence and reveal hidden structure.

\section{Summation as Analytic Continuation}

Every trigonometric series is secretly the boundary trace of a function
analytic in a disk. Let

\startformula
F(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n z^n.
\stopformula

The value of the original series at $z=e^{ix}$ can often be interpreted
as the analytic continuation of $F(z)$, even when the boundary series
diverges. Thus summation becomes a manifestation of holomorphic structure,
not numerical addition.

\section{Spectral Interpretation: Frequency Selection}

Via the shift operator

\startformula
(Tf)(x) = f(x+1),
\stopformula

whose eigenfunctions are $e^{inx}$, trigonometric summation becomes
a spectral selection problem. Summing a series corresponds to constructing
a function via assembly of eigenmodes under spectral constraints.


Each reveals a different world in which \quotation{the sum} possesses a
different meaning. A trigonometric series is a prism through which the
infinite reveals many faces.
The esoteric theory of summation shows that infinite oscillations can
produce finite truths, divergent expressions can acquire meaningful
interpretations, and analytic structure hides behind periodicity until
revealed through summation techniques. This chapter is an invitation to
embrace these deeper harmonies within trigonometry.
